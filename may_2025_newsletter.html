A Month of Mayhem, a Future Uncertain

In May, the world quietly began arranging itself for something worse.

The Re-Emergence of Trump's Travel Ban

The return of the Trump travel ban exemplified this, showcasing not merely a policy restoration but a posture of strength as cruelty, and security as selective exclusion. Though narrower than 2017’s infamous iteration, its message remained clear: some people belong, others do not.

Inertia and Centrist Leadership in Europe & Australia

Across the Atlantic, the German parliament handed Friedrich Merz the chancellorship, a victory for inertia in a Europe bracing for economic contraction. Merz, a centrist technocrat, offers managerial austerity in lieu of actual vision. A similar narrative played out in Australia, where the Labor Party eked out another term by mastering the art of governing without causing much stir.

Global Health Concerns and Domestic Distractions

But the surface calm belies deeper fractures.

In Malawi, health officials confirmed a sixth case of mpox, with no clear source. In the Philippines, the ruling alliance won its general election with thin credibility. In the United States, federal legislation against revenge porn was rightly hailed as a moral necessity, though it landed with the pageantry of a PR stunt, overshadowed by TikTok bans and new gun massacres.

The Contradictory Nature of Modern Rights

The result is a curious dissonance: rights granted and revoked in the same breath. Citizenship is stripped in one country, restored in another. First-world parliaments performatively rebuke authoritarianism while their own borders harden, surveillance states expand, and labor markets grow more precarious.

The Alarming Silence of Global Institutions

Nothing captures May’s absurdity like the silence — the eerie quiet from global institutions and media as the machinery of repression whirs back to life, updated, more efficient. Trump’s policies return not with shock, but with scheduling. Protesters march, but cameras are mostly elsewhere.

Anticipating June: A Future of Symbolic Gestures

By June, we anticipate more symbolic legislation, more abstract peace summits, and more praise for political “resilience” while communities unravel under economic exploitation, ecological pressure, and digital alienation.

A Crisis of Permitted Authoritarianism

What emerges is not merely a crisis of leadership, but a crisis of permission. The liberal order is learning to live with authoritarian impulses, so long as they come dressed in procedure and performed regret. The fascists have the advantage, not because they’re stronger, but because they’re clearer.

The People Bear the Burden of Clarity

As ever, the burden of clarity falls on the people — undocumented workers organizing in New York, nurses in Lilongwe tracing disease outbreaks, young voters in Manila demanding more than recycled strongmen.

The Urgent Question: Building the Future Ground

The question for June is not whether the world is tipping. It already has. The real question is: who’s going to build the ground we land on?
